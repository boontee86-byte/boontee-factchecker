A Crisis That is Bigger than the US-Iran War Could Explode Soon!

1M65 · Checked Mar 17, 2026
Overall Verdict
MOSTLY TRUE

Summary

This live stream by 1M65 (Mr. Loo) discusses the private credit market as a potential systemic financial crisis, comparing it to the 2007-2008 global financial crisis. The video's core thesis — that the $3.5 trillion private credit market faces serious stress from loan maturities, rising interest rates, AI disruption of software borrowers, and the concurrent oil crisis from the US-Iran conflict — is broadly supported by current financial reporting. Major private credit funds have indeed been gating redemptions in early 2026, and the confluence of these risks is a genuine concern raised by financial analysts and regulators.

However, the video contains some inaccuracies in specific figures (Singapore's GDP, the Fed nominee's name) and tends toward sensationalism by framing the situation as an inevitable catastrophe rather than a risk scenario. The comparison to the 2007-2008 crisis, while illustrative, overstates the current situation since private credit is smaller relative to the mortgage market that caused the GFC. The claim that the Federal Reserve is completely 'powerless' oversimplifies the Fed's toolkit. Overall, the educational content about private credit mechanics is sound, and the warning about systemic risks is timely and largely fact-based.

"The private credit market is $3.5 trillion in size"
TRUE
The Alternative Credit Council (ACC) and AIMA confirmed the global private credit market reached US$3.5 trillion AUM. Morgan Stanley estimates it could grow to $5 trillion by 2029. This figure is widely cited across financial publications.

"This private credit crisis is potentially comparable to the 2007-2008 global financial crisis"
MISLEADING
While there are structural parallels (opaque instruments, interconnected financial system, leverage), the private credit market at $3.5 trillion is significantly smaller than the $10+ trillion US mortgage market that triggered the 2008 crisis. The banking system is better capitalized today due to post-GFC reforms. However, the combination with the oil crisis and inflationary pressures does create a uniquely challenging environment. Many analysts describe it as a serious risk but not yet at GFC scale.
https://www.cnbc.com/amp/2026/01/23/wall-street-private-credit-risk-rising.htmlhttps://fortune.com/2026/03/14/private-credit-meltdown-how-wall-streets-blackstone-kkr-apollo-ares-blue-owl-investment-craze-panic/
The comparison is used for illustration but risks sensationalism. The presenter does qualify with 'potentially' and 'depending how it pans out,' which is more measured than the video title suggests.
11:46
"Private credit is loans made directly to companies by non-bank investors, bypassing public bond markets and traditional bank channels, and is not regulated"
MOSTLY TRUE
Private credit is indeed direct lending by non-bank entities. The Federal Reserve has published notes describing private credit's characteristics and noting the opacity of the market. However, saying it is 'not regulated' is an overstatement — private credit funds are subject to SEC oversight (for registered funds), and the OFR and Fed have been increasing scrutiny. It is less regulated than banking, but not unregulated.
https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/private-credit-characteristics-and-risks-20240223.html
The SEC has been increasing oversight, and the OFR published a brief in March 2026 specifically on measuring counterparty exposures to private credit.

"Private credit exploded after 2008 because post-GFC regulations forced banks to reduce risky lending and increase capital buffers"
TRUE
This is a well-documented phenomenon. Post-GFC regulations (Basel III, Dodd-Frank) significantly restricted bank leverage lending, creating a gap that private credit filled. The market grew from roughly $500 billion to $3.5 trillion in the years since. This is confirmed by the Federal Reserve, Morgan Stanley, and numerous financial research publications.

"Private credit firms offer investors 8-12% returns and lend out at 18% or higher, with borrowing costs of 12-18% for borrowers"
MISLEADING
The investor return range of 8-12% is broadly accurate for private credit. However, borrowing costs of 12-18% are on the high end. Industry benchmarks show all-in borrowing costs for quality middle-market credits around 9-11%, with riskier borrowers reaching 12%+. The claim of 18% rates would apply only to the riskiest distressed borrowers or when including all fees and PIK interest. The 'loan shark' comparison is colorful but exaggerated for most of the market.
https://privatecapitalglobal.com/blog/private-capital-debt-benchmarks-for-the-new-rate-environmenthttps://www.troweprice.com/en/us/ocredit/insights/private-credits-persistent-premium
Typical senior/unitranche pricing is SOFR + 425-575 bps, putting all-in rates at 9-11% for standard deals. The 18% figure would be unusual except for highly distressed or subordinated credit.

"Private credit firms allow 6-7x EBITDA leverage for borrowers"
MOSTLY TRUE
S&P Global Ratings found average leverage hit 7x in 2024 for US middle market credits. Most transactions now close around 5.0x, but adjusted EBITDA addbacks can push true leverage to 7-9x on actual cash EBITDA. During 2018-2021, 6x+ leverage was common. The range mentioned is historically accurate, though current standards have tightened somewhat.
https://www.spglobal.com/ratings/en/regulatory/article/250424-u-s-leveraged-finance-q1-2025-update-private-credit-boom-narrows-gap-to-bsl-market-s13476410https://www.abfjournal.com/leverage-limits-stress-testing-middle-market-debt-capacity-in-a-volatile-2025-economy/
True leverage depends heavily on EBITDA adjustments. Reported leverage of 5x may be 7-9x on unadjusted cash EBITDA.

30:54
"The Federal Reserve is powerless to solve this problem because in an inflationary environment they cannot cut rates to support private credit without fueling inflation further"
MOSTLY TRUE
The basic policy dilemma described is accurate — this is the classic 'stagflation trap.' If oil-driven inflation persists, the Fed faces a genuine conflict between fighting inflation (requiring higher rates) and preventing financial instability (requiring lower rates or liquidity support). However, calling the Fed 'powerless' overstates the case. The Fed has other tools beyond interest rates: targeted lending facilities, emergency liquidity programs, and regulatory forbearance. During 2023, the Fed created the BTFP to address banking stress while still raising rates.
https://fortune.com/2026/03/14/private-credit-meltdown-how-wall-streets-blackstone-kkr-apollo-ares-blue-owl-investment-craze-panic/https://dailyreckoning.com/loans-to-nonbanks-threaten-banking-crisis/
The Fed's dual mandate creates real tension in this scenario, but history shows the Fed can create targeted programs. The 'powerless' framing is an overstatement, though the underlying concern is legitimate.
