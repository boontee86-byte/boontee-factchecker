Mr. Loo's crash buying strategy is one of the most debated investment frameworks in Singapore's retail investing community. Developed by Loo Cheng Chuan — better known as "Mr. CPF," the founder of the 1M65 Movement — the strategy offers a rules-based, psychologically engineered method for deploying capital during stock market crashes. Rather than trying to predict when crashes will occur, the system pre-commits investors to a tiered deployment schedule triggered by specific percentage drawdowns from all-time highs. The result, its proponents argue, is a disciplined approach that turns market panics into structured wealth-building events.

This primer covers the full picture: who Mr. Loo is and where the strategy came from, the mechanical rules in detail, the asset selection philosophy, historical backtested results, and the robust academic and community debate that has surrounded the strategy since it was formalised in a widely discussed ten-lesson series published in late 2025 and early 2026.

Who Is Mr. Loo Cheng Chuan?

Loo Cheng Chuan is a Singaporean entrepreneur, investor, and financial educator born around 1973. He holds a graduate qualification from the Stanford Sloan Programme — an executive education programme equivalent to a senior MBA — and spent years as an executive at Singtel, one of Singapore's largest telecommunications companies, before transitioning to entrepreneurship. He and his wife run Premiummall, an e-commerce personal shopping business launched in 2014.

He is most widely known as the founder of the 1M65 Movement, a nonprofit initiative aimed at helping Singaporean couples accumulate S$1 million in their CPF (Central Provident Fund) accounts by age 65. The name is shorthand for "One Million by 65." He and his wife achieved this milestone at age 45 — twenty years ahead of schedule — earning him the Public Sector Transformation Award in 2018, one of the few non-civil servants to receive the honour.

His financial philosophy was shaped by a formative early loss. At age 24, Loo lost over S$100,000 through speculative investing. His father, a primary school teacher, bailed him out. He has described the experience as transformative: "That kind of blow was very good, because it woke up my life." It redirected him from speculation toward disciplined, rules-based wealth accumulation — the philosophy that underlies everything he teaches today.

Mr. Loo is active on YouTube (channel: 1M65), Facebook, and Instagram, and has delivered more than 71 public speaking engagements, all unpaid. He has advised startups including Endowus, Gushcloud, and 2359 Media.

The Core Philosophy: Crashes as Engineered Opportunities

The foundation of Mr. Loo's crash buying strategy is a reframing of market crashes — from threats to be survived, into pre-planned wealth acceleration events.

The insight is not original to Loo. Warren Buffett captured the same idea in a 2008 New York Times op-ed: "The best chance to deploy capital is when things are going down." Buffett's principle — "Be fearful when others are greedy and greedy when others are fearful" — is the philosophical ancestor of the strategy. What Mr. Loo contributes is a formalised, mechanical system that removes the psychological barrier to executing that principle in real time.

His central argument is that investing is not primarily a mathematical exercise — it is a psychological endurance test. Most investors know they should buy during crashes. Almost none of them do, because fear, uncertainty, and the absence of a pre-committed plan paralyse them at exactly the moment decisive action is required. The crash buying strategy's primary innovation is structural: it is designed, calibrated, and committed to before the crash arrives, so that execution becomes nearly automatic when markets fall.

As Loo has put it: "There is no emotion involved. Just blindly follow according to this rule."

The Mechanism: How the Strategy Works

Two Pools of Capital

The strategy divides an investor's portfolio into two distinct pools:

  • Pool 1 — Regular Investment: Capital deployed continuously through dollar-cost averaging (DCA) into broad market index funds, regardless of market conditions. This pool never stops working.
  • Pool 2 — The "War Chest" (Dry Powder): A dedicated cash reserve held specifically for crash deployment. This cash does not go into the market under normal conditions. It sits idle, waiting.

The war chest is not an emergency fund. It is not opportunistic savings. It is a formally earmarked allocation with a pre-specified deployment schedule.

The Tiered Deployment Schedule

The strategy's operational core is a five-tier deployment grid, triggered by successive percentage declines from the market's all-time high (ATH). Using a S$200,000 war chest as the worked example:

Crash Buying Deployment Schedule (S$200,000 War Chest)
Market Down 10% — Deploy 10%
S$20,000
Market Down 15% — Deploy 15%
S$30,000
Market Down 20% — Deploy 20%
S$40,000
Market Down 25% — Deploy 25%
S$50,000
Market Down 30% — Deploy 30%
S$60,000

Each tier fires independently. If the market drops 10% and then recovers, only Tier 1 has deployed. If it falls all the way to 30%, the entire war chest is deployed across five tranches. The investor does not need to guess where the bottom is — the rules handle execution at every level.

Earlier versions of the strategy (as described in a DollarsAndSense interview) started deployment at 20–25% declines and divided the war chest into 5–10 "bullets" deployed once every one to two weeks during the drawdown. The more recent ten-lesson formalisation begins deploying at the 10% mark, extending the range of participation downward.

Timing Within Each Tier

Within a given drop tier, Loo recommends spreading purchases across time — buying once every one to two weeks rather than all at once. This provides further averaging within the tier and reflects that crashes rarely move in straight lines. Markets often continue falling after an initial drop, and spacing out purchases within a tier can improve the average entry price.

Asset Selection: What to Buy During Crashes

Mr. Loo is explicit and consistent about what belongs in the crash buying portfolio: broad-based index funds, primarily tracking the S&P 500 or the Nasdaq 100.

He does not recommend:

  • Individual stocks
  • Singapore-focused funds (e.g., the Straits Times Index)
  • Speculative assets or sector bets
  • Bond funds (for this component of the portfolio)

His rationale for the S&P 500 specifically rests on its long-term recovery record. He cites the example: had an investor bought the S&P 500 at the absolute peak before the 2008 Global Financial Crisis and held through the subsequent crash, they would still have achieved a 456.82% cumulative gain by 2023. The index's thirty-year track record demonstrates that even investors with disastrously poor timing — buying at every peak — still generated 7–9% annualised returns over long holding periods.

For Singapore-based investors, Loo points to practical access routes:

  • SGX-listed: Lion-Phillip S-REIT ETF or similar (though his primary focus is on S&P 500 exposure)
  • US-listed: Vanguard's VOO, BlackRock's IVV, or State Street's SPY
  • Platforms: Interactive Brokers, Saxo, FSMOne for low-cost US ETF access from Singapore

The portfolio allocation alongside crash buying includes a Nasdaq 100 index fund component, consistent with Loo's preference for US tech-weighted exposure as a complement to the broader S&P 500.

Historical Backtesting: Three Crashes, Three Results

Mr. Loo built a Crash Buying Simulator to demonstrate how the strategy would have performed across three distinct market dislocations between 2018 and 2026, using a S$200,000 war chest:

The 2018 US-China Trade War Correction

The S&P 500 fell approximately 20% from its 2018 peak during the trade war escalation. Three tiers deployed (10%, 15%, 20%). By early 2026, the estimated profit from this deployment: approximately S$198,000 — nearly doubling the deployed capital over approximately seven years.

The 2020 COVID-19 Crash

The COVID crash was the cleanest demonstration of the full strategy. The S&P 500 fell approximately 34% from its February 2020 peak to the March 2020 trough — all five tiers triggered. Loo executed this in real time, deploying once every one to two weeks throughout March 2020. By early 2026, the estimated profit from the full five-tier deployment: approximately S$348,000.

Loo has stated he personally "made millions" during the COVID crash, with his total profits from CPF and SRS investing since 2020 exceeding S$720,000 at approximately 15% annualised returns.

The 2022 Inflation/Rate Hike Bear Market

The 2022 bear market — driven by aggressive Federal Reserve rate hikes — brought the S&P 500 down approximately 25% from its peak. Four tiers deployed; the 30% tier was not triggered. Estimated profit by early 2026: approximately S$127,000.

Portfolio Architecture: Where Crash Buying Fits

Crash buying is not Mr. Loo's complete investment philosophy — it is one component of a layered wealth-building architecture. He describes four layers, which must be built in sequence, each providing security before the next layer is added:

Layer 1 — Foundation (CPF/1M65): Maximise CPF contributions to earn the government-guaranteed 2.5–4% returns. This is the bedrock — capital that cannot be lost. The 1M65 framework targets CPF millionaire status. Once secured, the investor has a permanent base that supports risk-taking above it.

Layer 2 — Core Equity (Crash Buying + DCA): S&P 500 and Nasdaq 100 index funds, deployed through continuous DCA (Pool 1) and the crash buying war chest (Pool 2). This is the primary wealth-compounding engine.

Layer 3 — Algorithmic Trading: Limited to a maximum 15% of total portfolio. Quantitative or rules-based trading strategies as a satellite allocation.

Layer 4 — High Risk/High Return: Hedge fund or alternative allocations. Only viable after the lower three layers are secured.

The crash buying strategy lives in Layer 2. It is explicitly not for capital that should be in Layer 1. Loo has emphasised that the war chest must be money the investor can afford to have tied up and potentially mark-to-market negative during an extended downturn.

The Crash Buying vs. DCA vs. Lump Sum Debate

Since Loo published his ten-lesson crash buying series in late 2025, the strategy has been the subject of one of Singapore's most active retail investing debates — drawing in YouTubers, bloggers, and financial commentators.

The Academic Baseline

The evidence against crash buying (or any form of waiting-to-deploy strategy) is substantial:

  • Vanguard Research: Lump-sum investing outperforms DCA approximately 68% of the time in the US, 66% globally. When DCA periods extend to 36 months, lump sum outperforms in approximately 90% of rolling periods. The average incremental benefit of lump sum over DCA is 1.5–2.4%.
  • AQR Research: Waiting to buy the dip results in 18.7% lower ending wealth compared to straightforward DCA across tested implementations. Of 196 implementations tested, only 16 (8%) showed statistically significant alpha, with an average excess return of approximately 0.5% per year.
  • S&P Global (60-year study): Buy-the-dip strategies underperform passive investing more than 60% of the time over six decades of data.
  • DeBondt & Thaler (1985): The foundational academic paper on contrarian investing found "loser" stocks outperformed "winner" stocks by approximately 30% cumulatively over 36 months — providing partial support for crash buying in individual stocks, though transaction costs may absorb the gain (Jegadeesh & Titman, 1993).

The Singapore Debate (Early 2026)

Singapore financial YouTubers Boon Tee and Kelvin Learns Investing published analyses arguing that DCA or lump sum outperform crash buying when the data window extends back to 1981 — reversing the more favourable results seen in Loo's 2018–2026 simulator.

Mr. Loo's counter-arguments:

1. Data window selection: The 2018–2026 period is more representative of present market conditions than 1981 data, which was captured under fundamentally different interest rate, monetary policy, and technological environments.

2. Real-world executability: Lump sum wins on spreadsheets but most real investors cannot psychologically execute it — they freeze or sell at the bottom. A strategy you can actually follow through a crash is worth more than a theoretically optimal one you'll abandon.

3. Practical track record: His personal returns (~15% annualised, S$720,000+ in profits) demonstrate real-world execution where many who theoretically hold lump sum portfolios sell in panic.

4. The "68%" rebuttal: He argues the Vanguard lump sum finding contains hidden statistical biases and does not account for the behavioural reality of investor psychology.

Loo publicly called his critics "paper warriors" (纸上谈兵 — a Chinese idiom for those with theoretical knowledge but no practical experience). A live video debate was organised in February 2026 bringing together Mr. Loo, Boon Tee, Kelvin, and other Singapore financial commentators to examine the competing frameworks directly.

The Emerging Synthesis

The debate has produced a working compromise position among many Singapore retail investors: a hybrid approach. Run regular DCA continuously (capturing the mathematical efficiency of time-in-market investing), while maintaining a modest war chest (15–20% of equity allocation) dedicated to crash deployment. This hybrid captures both the baseline compounding of continuous DCA and the opportunistic acceleration of crash buying, without requiring the investor to hold large idle cash positions during long bull markets.

Criticisms and Limitations

Cash Drag

The most quantitatively robust criticism is cash drag — the opportunity cost of holding an uninvested war chest during the long bull markets between crashes. If an investor maintains a S$200,000 war chest that earns 2–3% in a high-yield savings account or T-bills while the S&P 500 compounds at 10–12% annually, they are sacrificing 7–9% per year on that capital for years at a time.

Between major crashes — which can be separated by 3–7+ years — the drag compounds significantly. The AQR finding that buy-the-dip produces 18.7% lower ending wealth reflects precisely this cost.

Market Timing Risk

Crash buying is, at its core, a form of market timing — the deployment of capital is contingent on market conditions reaching specified thresholds. Academic finance has accumulated decades of evidence that market timing destroys value for most investors. While Mr. Loo's rules-based approach is considerably more disciplined than discretionary market timing, it still requires the market to fall to trigger deployment, and if crashes are shallow or brief, the war chest may remain largely undeployed.

Survivorship Bias in Historical Examples

The three crashes Loo cites (2018, 2020, 2022) all resolved with substantial recoveries within his backtesting window ending in 2026. A strategy tested on a period that includes a rapid V-shaped recovery (particularly the 2020 COVID crash, which recovered within months) may overstate typical results. Investors who deployed the strategy in, say, Japan in 1990 — where the Nikkei took over 30 years to recover its previous high — would have experienced very different outcomes.

Optimisation to the Test Window

Critics note that using 2018–2026 as the primary backtest window cherry-picks a highly favourable period: a strong US equity bull market punctuated by three crashes that all recovered sharply. The 1981–2024 window used by critics produces less favourable results for the strategy.

What Happens If Tiers Never Trigger?

During extended bull markets — the S&P 500 gained over 500% from 2009 to 2020 without a 20% correction for most of that period — the war chest sits idle for years. The psychological benefit of "being ready" must be weighed against the mathematical cost of uninvested capital over a decade-plus bull run.

Practical Implementation for Investors

For investors interested in implementing the crash buying strategy, several practical considerations apply:

Setting War Chest Size: Loo's worked examples use S$200,000, but the strategy scales proportionally. What matters is the ratio of war chest to total equity portfolio. Most commentators suggest 10–20% of total equity exposure as a reasonable war chest allocation — enough to be meaningful during crashes without excessive idle cash drag.

Where to Hold the War Chest: Options include high-yield savings accounts, Singapore Savings Bonds (SSBs), T-bills, or money market funds. The goal is capital preservation with modest yield while maintaining liquidity for rapid deployment.

Platform Selection for Execution: For S&P 500 index funds accessible to Singapore investors:

  • Interactive Brokers: Lowest commissions for US-listed ETFs (VOO, IVV, SPY)
  • Saxo: Higher commissions but local Singapore entity, strong platform
  • FSMOne: Straightforward interface, competitive for regular purchases
  • Endowus (SRS/CPF): For tax-advantaged accounts specifically

Monitoring Triggers: The strategy requires tracking the S&P 500's distance from its all-time high. Free tools (Yahoo Finance, Google Finance, Bloomberg) provide this. The investor pre-sets calendar alerts or regularly checks to identify when tiers trigger.

Behaviour During Deployment: When a tier triggers, execute promptly and resist the urge to "wait for a lower price." The point of the tiered system is that you have already pre-committed to buying at each level — second-guessing the rules defeats their purpose.

Broader Academic Context: Does Buying Crashes Work?

Professor Aswath Damodaran of NYU's Stern School of Business categorises contrarian investing (including crash buying) into four types, ranked by evidence quality:

1. Knee-jerk Contrarianism — Buy anything that has fallen sharply. Weakest evidence; noise traders buying random losers.

2. Technical Contrarianism — Buy based on price patterns and defined drawdown thresholds (closest to Mr. Loo's approach). Mixed evidence.

3. Constrained Contrarianism — Buy cheap stocks that meet quality screens. Moderate evidence.

4. Opportunistic Contrarianism — Buy assets during genuine market dislocations using fundamental analysis (Buffett's actual approach). Best evidence but hardest to execute.

Mr. Loo's index fund crash buying most closely resembles Damodaran's second category — technical contrarianism with pre-set percentage thresholds. The evidence for this specific form is mixed: better than random contrarianism, weaker than fundamental value-based contrarian approaches.

The strategy's strongest academic support comes from the behavioural finance literature: investors who have a pre-committed mechanical system consistently outperform investors who make discretionary decisions during market stress, primarily because discretionary investors sell at bottoms (Brad Barber & Terrance Odean, 2000; Dalbar QAIB studies showing average investor returns trail the market by 3–4% annually largely due to behavioural timing errors).

The 1M65 Strategy in Context: CPF as the Foundation

To understand crash buying as Loo intends it, it must be understood within the broader 1M65 framework. The strategy's core sequence:

1. First: Maximise CPF contributions to build a guaranteed, government-backed foundation (2.5–4% risk-free, compounding over decades).

2. Then: Deploy crash buying using capital above and beyond what is needed for CPF.

3. The war chest comes from surplus: Not from emergency funds, not from CPF, not from capital needed for near-term goals.

This sequencing is important. Critics who evaluate crash buying in isolation — as a standalone strategy against lump sum or DCA — are assessing it differently from how Loo presents it. In his framework, it is an acceleration mechanism for investors who already have a secure foundation, not a replacement for conventional index fund investing.

---

Conclusion: What the Strategy Gets Right, and Where It Falls Short

Mr. Loo's crash buying strategy makes a genuinely important contribution to retail investor education: it takes the abstract principle of "buy when others are fearful" and converts it into a concrete, executable system with defined rules, defined triggers, and defined deployment amounts. The psychological engineering is real — investors who have pre-committed to the rules are meaningfully more likely to execute during panics than those who are making discretionary decisions under stress.

The strategy's limitations are also real. The academic evidence consistently shows that holding cash outside the market costs compounding returns, that lump sum investing outperforms staged deployment approximately two-thirds of the time, and that the 2018–2026 backtest window used in Loo's simulator was exceptionally favourable. Investors in markets that experience slow recoveries, prolonged drawdowns, or sustained bear markets may find the cash drag dominates the crash-buying gains.

The most defensible application of the framework appears to be the hybrid approach many Singapore commentators have converged on: maintain continuous DCA as the primary strategy, and hold a modest dedicated war chest (10–20% of equity allocation) for tiered crash deployment — capturing the mathematical efficiency of time-in-market investing while retaining the ability to accelerate when genuine dislocations occur.

Whether one adopts the crash buying framework fully, partially, or rejects it in favour of simple DCA, Loo's broader message is worth internalising: prepare before the crash, not during it. The investor who has a plan when markets fall will almost always outperform the investor who improvises under panic — regardless of which specific strategy the plan contains.
